Climate Change Data for Project 4

Information on global climate change, sourced from the Environmental Protection Agency, NASA’s Global Climate Change initiative, and the National Wildlife Federation, that you can use to construct your information design-focused motion graphic.

Feel free (though NOT required) to add to this with any other well-sourced, well-researched information of your own, or to rephrase the content provided below (so long as the information itself is not misrepresented).

How it works

  1. Greenhouse gases act like a blanket around Earth, trapping energy in the atmosphere and causing it to warm. This phenomenon is called the greenhouse effect and is natural and necessary to support life on Earth. However, the buildup of greenhouse gases can change Earth’s climate and result in dangerous effects to human health and welfare and to ecosystems.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/climate-change-basic-information
  2. Greenhouse gases include carbon dioxide, methane and water vapor.
    Source(s): www.nwf.org/Eco-Schools-USA/Become-an-Eco-School/Pathways/Climate-Change/Facts.aspx
  3. Burning one gallon of gasoline puts 19.64 pounds of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere.
    Source(s): www.nwf.org/Eco-Schools-USA/Become-an-Eco-School/Pathways/Climate-Change/Facts.aspx
  4. Scientists have known since the 1800s that greenhouse gases trap heat, preventing it from escaping to space. The warming effect of greenhouse gases is amplified by feedbacks, especially from water vapor (a powerful and plentiful natural greenhouse gas), leading to more warming and changes in climate.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  5. The atmospheric concentration of carbon dioxide (CO2) has increased by more than 40% since pre-industrial times, and the current CO2 level is higher than it has been in at least 800,000 years.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  6. Natural influences on climate, such as changes in solar radiation, natural cycles, volcanic eruptions, and the climate’s normal year-to-year variability, can’t fully explain the current warming trend. The climate changes observed in recent decades follow a number of patterns—such as cooling at high altitudes and more warming at night than during the day—that are consistent with what scientists would expect from an increase in greenhouse gases rather than changes in solar variability or other natural causes.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  7. Sea level rise is caused primarily by two factors related to climate change: the added water from melting land ice and the expansion of sea water as it warms.
    Source(s): climate.nasa.gov/vital-signs/sea-level/

Global outcomes

  1. 2016 was the warmest year on record, continuing a decades-long warming trend in global surface temperature.
    Source(s): climate.nasa.gov/vital-signs/global-temperature/
  2. Earth’s 2016 surface temperatures were the warmest since modern record-keeping began in 1880, according to independent analyses by NASA and the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA).
    Source(s): climate.nasa.gov/news/2537/nasa-noaa-data-show-2016-warmest-year-on-record-globally/
  3. 2016 was the third year in a row to set a new record for global average surface temperatures.
    Source(s): climate.nasa.gov/news/2537/nasa-noaa-data-show-2016-warmest-year-on-record-globally/
  4. The 2016 temperatures continue a long-term warming trend, according to analyses by scientists at NASA’s Goddard Institute for Space Studies (GISS) in New York. NOAA scientists concur with the finding that 2016 was the warmest year on record based on separate, independent analyses of the data.
    Source(s): climate.nasa.gov/news/2537/nasa-noaa-data-show-2016-warmest-year-on-record-globally/
  5. The Earth’s average temperature has risen by 1.5°F over the past century, and climate scientists estimate it will rise another 0.5 to 8.6°F by the end of this century, depending, in part, on future emissions. Small changes in the average temperature of the planet can translate to large and potentially dangerous shifts in climate and weather.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/climate-change-basic-information
    www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  6. The global average temperature during the height of the last ice age was only 5 to 9°F cooler than it is today.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  7. Over the past century, human activities have released large amounts of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. The majority of greenhouse gases come from burning fossil fuels to produce energy, although deforestation, industrial processes, and some agricultural practices also emit gases into the atmosphere.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/climate-change-basic-information
  8. Rising global temperatures have been accompanied by changes in weather and climate such as changes in rainfall, resulting in more floods, droughts, or intense rain, as well as more frequent and severe heat waves.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/climate-change-basic-information
  9. Climate change has caused the planet’s oceans and glaciers to experience some big changes – oceans are warming and becoming more acidic, ice caps are melting, and sea levels are rising.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/climate-change-basic-information
  10. NASA’s latest satellite observations show global average sea level rising currently at 3.4 mm per year. It has risen 8 inches since 1880; it is projected to rise another 1 to 4 feet by 2100.
    Source(s): climate.nasa.gov/vital-signs/sea-level/
  11. Especially dramatic changes are underway in the Arctic, where warming is amplified by powerful feedbacks. Reductions in sea ice, land-based ice, and snow cover, along with the thawing of permafrost, are having profound impacts in the Arctic and beyond.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  12. Rising sea levels, caused mainly by the expansion of seawater as it warms, along with billions of tons of water added to the ocean each year from melting glaciers, ice caps, and ice sheets, are affecting coastal communities in many parts of the world, including places like South Florida, Chesapeake Bay, and low-lying communities along the Gulf Coast in the United States.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  13. Climate change is increasing our exposure to extreme temperatures, extreme weather events; degraded air quality; diseases transmitted through food, water, and insects; and stresses to mental health and well-being. These threats to human health are expected to increase with continued climate change.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  14. The United States is the second largest contributor to CO2 in our atmosphere, though it is home to just 4.4 percent of the world’s population. If everyone in the world lived the way people do in the U.S., it would take four Earths to provide enough resources for everyone.
    Source(s): www.nwf.org/Eco-Schools-USA/Become-an-Eco-School/Pathways/Climate-Change/Facts.aspx
    www.worldatlas.com/
    www.census.gov/popclock/
    www.popsci.com/environment
  15. The amount of carbon dioxide emitted per capita in the United States is 16.4 metric tons (almost 40 pounds per person).
    Source(s): www.nwf.org/Eco-Schools-USA/Become-an-Eco-School/Pathways/Climate-Change/Facts.aspx
    databank.worldbank.org/data
  16. The current pace of global average temperature rise puts approximately 25-35% of plant and animal species at increased risk of extinction.
    Source(s): www.nwf.org/Eco-Schools-USA/Become-an-Eco-School/Pathways/Climate-Change/Facts.aspx

Local outcomes

  1. New Jersey has warmed by about 3°F in the last century. This and other climate impacts, like more intense rainfall, mean increasing risks to health. Hurricanes and storms like nor’easters may become more intense in New Jersey as the climate continues to warm.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climate-impacts/climate-change-and-human-health-risks-your-state
  2. New Jersey has warmed by about 3°F in the last century. This and other climate impacts, like more intense rainfall, mean increasing risks to health. Higher temperatures and other weather changes will increase levels of ozone pollution (a component of smog), increasing health risks like asthma-related hospital visits.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climate-impacts/climate-change-and-human-health-risks-your-state
  3. New Jersey has warmed by about 3°F in the last century. This and other climate impacts, like more intense rainfall, mean increasing risks to health. Longer warm seasons mean that ticks carrying Lyme disease will emerge earlier in the season and move into new areas.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climate-impacts/climate-change-and-human-health-risks-your-state
  4. Heat waves have become more frequent in the United States in recent decades. Climate scientists expect the number of days with temperatures above 90°F to increase in the United States as the climate changes, especially toward the end of this century.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  5. Higher temperatures lead to increased rates of evaporation and can lead to more rapid drying of soils. Without reductions in global greenhouse gas emissions, longer-term droughts are expected to intensify in much of the Southwest, the Great Plains, and the Southeast.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  6. The area burned by wildfire in parts of western North America is expected to double (or more) for each 1.8°F increase in global average temperature.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  7. Because of rising global sea levels, flooding is becoming more frequent along the U.S. coastline, especially in the Mid-Atlantic region where the land is simultaneously sinking.
    Source(s): www.epa.gov/climatechange/frequently-asked-questions-about-climate-change
  8. In 1910 Glacier National Park was home to more an estimated 150 glaciers. That number has now shrunk to 25 as of December 2016. This national park is expected to eventually lose all its glaciers.
    Source(s): www.nwf.org/Eco-Schools-USA/Become-an-Eco-School/Pathways/Climate-Change/Facts.aspx
    www.nps.gov/glac/learn/education/glaciers.htm
