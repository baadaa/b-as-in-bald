Project 3: The Reaction Gif

Animated GIFs, a file format introduced in 1987, are not new technology. Still, they've been given new life in contemporary internet culture. They are portable (due to their small file size and wide support across many platforms), accessible (easy for most internet users to find, digest, and even make themselves), and their quick, looping, bite-size format lends itself well to humor and wit. All of this makes the animated GIF a popular form of online visual communication.

Animated GIFs are not a medium that generally involves a long, laborious design process. Part of what makes them interesting and unique is that both the concept development and the actual act of making is often quick and rough, as well as exceptionally open. Making animated GIFs is not something limited to professional or serious designers, and they have opened the world of visual communication and motion design to many people. Anyone with access to a computer and the internet can easily make funny, interesting, effective animated GIFs from found images or video footage, from their own original footage, or by altering/combining existing GIFs.

This project specifically focuses on the idea of the reaction gif. From the description of The Reaction GIF: Moving Image as Gesture, an exhibition at the Museum of the Moving Image:

…over the last few years, the reaction GIF has emerged as a form for communicating with short moving images in response to, and often in lieu of, text in online forums and comment threads. These animated GIFs consist of brief loops of bodies in motion, primarily excerpted from recognizable pop culture moments, and are used to express common ideas and emotions. Understood as gestures, they can communicate more nuance and concision than their verbal translations.

For this project you will create three unique GIFs. They can communicate any idea(s) or emotion(s) of your choosing.

  1. A completely original reaction GIF: you must make it entirely out of your own video footage, photography, illustration, animation, or whatever else you can come up with.
  2. Add to or change found material: start with a preexisting GIF, video, image(s), or animation and in some way alter it to communicate a specific reaction: you can add design elements, juxtapose your found material against different material, etc.
  3. No constraints for the third gif. It does not have to specifically be a reaction GIF and can be anything you’d like. Experiment!

I’ve set up a Tumblr to which all gifs created for this project should be submitted: Rutgers GIF Party. Submit all completed gifs to the Tumblr before the beginning of class on Monday, March 28 so that I have time to approve the posts. Note Tumblr’s max GIF size: no larger than 3MB and no more than 540 pixels wide.

Objectives

  • Investigate animated gifs as a form of time-based media / motion design
  • Investigate altering, remixing, and juxtaposing found media to communicate a new message
  • Practice rapid concept development and making
  • Continue to investigate the combination of traditional design attributes (color, composition, shape, depth, tension, contrast) and time-based attributes (progression, pacing, rhythm, sequence, velocity, kinetic behavior).
