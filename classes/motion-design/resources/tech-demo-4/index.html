3D Layers and Objects

  • So far we’ve—mostly—been working with flat, two-dimensional layers. After Effects also allows you to make a layer three-dimensional.
  • When you make a layer a 3D layer, it is still flat but will gain additional properties such as:
    • More axes for adjusting position, anchor point, scale, rotation
    • Material Options properties (which specify how the layer interacts with light and shadows)

How to make a 3D layer

  • Create a composition and add a layer as usual. (For this demo’s purposes I’m making a shape layer, but you can make 3D layers out of video footage, images, etc, as well.)
  • In your timeline, click on the 3D layer switch for your selected layer (looks like a 3D cube). The 3D properties should now be available to you.
  • Play around with the 3D Properties:
  • X, Y and Z rotation will now let you rotate your shape around 3 different axes. Orientation allows you to adjust your rotation in similar ways.
  • Note that position, anchor point and scale now have 3 numbers that you can set (because of the 3 axes)
  • You can add keyframes to any of these properties to animate
  • Notes:
    • The placement of your layer’s anchor point is more important than ever with 3D layers. Because your layer will move and rotate in three dimensions, not just two, around that anchor point.
    • The Material Options properties change the appearance and behavior of the layer, and can make it more realistic. Since this makes 3D layers even more complex, however, I won’t require you to work with material options but have provided a tutorial (linked below) should you wish to give it a try.

3D Null Objects:

  • You can animate a layer in 3D space by adding keyframes directly to that layer’s properties, OR you can do this by parenting a layer to a 3D null object.
  • Using a null object can help simplify a layer’s 3D motion, if it has a lot of properties and keyframes. It’s also helpful if you have several layers that you wish to move in 3D space together. Essentially, it’s similar to using null objects and parenting in 2D space.
  • A null object is basically a layer that contains no real, visible objects but only motion properties and keyframes.
  • To parent a layer to a 3D null layer:
    • Add a null layer above the layer that you want to parent to the 3D null object. For this demo, I’m using a simple shape layer.
    • Make BOTH layers 3D by clicking the 3D layer switch.
    • Click Toggle switches / modes in your timeline to view Modes.
    • Parent the shape layer to the null layer by clicking Parent dropdown in shape layer and choosing the null layer.
    • Adjust properties and add keyframes to the null layer to get the motion you want, and your shape layer should now match the animated properties of your null layer.
    • If you adjust your null layer’s keyframes, anchor point, position, etc, it will influence your shape layer. But adjusting properties or/and adding keyframes to your shape layer will not influence the null layer.

Camera Layers

  • When working with an individual 3D layer, you are making adjustments to that one specific layer in 3D space. Camera layers allow you to view 3D layers from any angle and distance. It’s moving the entire frame through which you see the composition in 3D space.
  • Camera layers only affect 3D layers.
  • How to use camera layers:
    • Make a composition and add several layers. Make ALL layers 3D, and turn on the Draft 3D mode for the entire composition by clicking on the 3D cube icon at the top of the timeline window. It should turn blue when enabled.
    • Add a Camera layer: Layer > New > Camera. You can leave the camera settings as-is for now.
    • Nothing noticeably changes after adding the camera layer, but play around with the Transform properties under the camera layer on the timeline.
    • Point of interest is where your camera is pointing at, and is essentially the anchor point for camera layers
    • Position is where the camera is located in space, and allows you to move front/back (Z-axis), up/down (Y-axis), and side-to-side (X-axis).
    • X, Y, and Z rotations allow you to pan your camera along different axes
    • Add keyframes to animate the camera transform properties
    • You can give the composition a sense of depth in 3D space by adjusting each individual layer’s 3D position while adjusting the camera layer’s position and rotation.
    • Note:
      • The order of your layers in your timeline is important when using camera layers. Try to put whatever’s on top in front, otherwise you may confuse After Effects.
      • Some people also like to use null objects / parenting with their camera layers to simplify animating the camera properties, which can be somewhat complex and a little fussy.

Advanced 3D Properties and Options

Once you’ve got a handle on the basic transform properties of the camera layers and 3D layers, you can use Material Option properties (for each specific 3D layer), Camera Options properties (for the camera layer), and Light layers to add additional realism and sense of depth, as well as camera/light effects that mimic the way a real camera would work.

This is fairly advanced stuff, and you are not required to tackle it since you already have plenty of technology and tools to wrap your heads around. That said, if you want to play around with material options, camera options and lighting, below are some tutorials/resources for your reference:

Character Rigging and Puppet Tool Basics:

Finally, After Effects is a powerful program capable of plenty of things we didn’t get into in this class because they’re tools more intended for animators/illustrators than graphic designers.

A brief intro to one tool used largely for character rigging (i.e. essentially bending a flat illustration so that it can move around) using something called the Puppet Tool:

  • In your timeline, select the layer containing the image you want to animate
  • In the menu at the top of your screen, click on the pin icon. This is the Puppet tool.
  • With the Pin selected, click on your character’s joints. These are the spots where you want bending to occur.
  • Once you’ve added pins you can grab and move them around (experiment!). The character moves fairly organically based off of the joints you marked.
  • In your timeline, this layer now has a Puppet dropdown menu under the Effects. Open Mesh 1 > Deform, and you’ll find each pin listed individually.
  • Open each pin’s sub-layer to see keyframes. After Effects has automatically added keyframes to start you off, so just scrub forward in your timeline and change the pin positions to animate. Additional keyframes are automatically added when you scrub forward and move pins.
  • Note: this is a fairly basic character rigging tool, so it tends to work best with fairly simple illustrations.
